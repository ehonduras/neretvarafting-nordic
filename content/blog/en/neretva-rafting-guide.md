Flying from London, Frankfurt, or Vienna to Sarajevo or Mostar takes roughly as long as a trip to the Mediterranean — but the landscape you land in is something else entirely. Green valleys, turquoise rivers, and mountains rising straight out of the gorges. If you are planning a Balkan trip and want an active day beyond city walks and café terraces, rafting on the Neretva is one of the best choices you can make.

## Why the Neretva?

The Neretva is Bosnia's longest river and flows through some of the country's most beautiful scenery. The section between Konjic and Glavatičevo offers a perfect mix of calmer stretches and more action-packed passages — class II to IV depending on water level and season. For travellers used to the outdoors but perhaps new to whitewater paddling, this is an ideal introduction: challenging enough to feel the adrenaline, but never overwhelming thanks to experienced guides.

Konjic is the natural starting point. The town sits roughly halfway between Sarajevo and Mostar, making it perfect for a day trip whichever direction you are travelling. Glavatičevo, where the river section begins, is a short drive upstream and surrounded by green mountains and crystal-clear water.

## Getting there from Europe

Most international travellers fly to Sarajevo via Vienna, Munich, or Istanbul. Direct flights are available seasonally from some hubs, but with a connection you usually land the afternoon before your rafting day. Alternatively, fly to Mostar if you plan to explore southern Bosnia first.

From Sarajevo it takes about 45 minutes by car to Konjic. From Mostar it is roughly one hour. Many operators offer transfer from both cities, which makes it easy if you are not hiring a car. Train connections also run along the route, but car or organised transfer is the smoothest option for a full day.

Plan to be at Glavatičevo early in the morning. A typical day starts around 09:00 with a safety briefing and equipment fitting.

## A day on the river

The day begins when you meet your guides — certified by the International Rafting Federation (IRF), meaning they follow international safety standards. You get help putting on a wetsuit, helmet, and life jacket. All equipment is included in the price.

After a thorough briefing you paddle out on the river. The first kilometres run through calmer water where you get used to the paddle and teamwork in the boat. Then come the fun sections — class II–IV rapids where the guide gives clear commands and the whole crew paddles together. Water level varies with the season: spring and early summer bring more action, while late summer is often somewhat calmer.

Lunch is served as a traditional Bosnian BBQ on the riverbank — grilled meat, fresh salad, bread, and local sides. It is a generous meal that keeps you going for the afternoon section. Breakfast is not included, so eat a proper breakfast at your hotel before departure.

After lunch the trip continues downstream toward Konjic. In total you paddle about 23 kilometres during the day. You are back in Konjic in the late afternoon, with plenty of time for dinner in town before returning to Sarajevo or Mostar.

## Prices and what's included

A full-day rafting trip on the Neretva costs around 50 EUR per person. The price includes all equipment (wetsuit, helmet, life jacket, paddle), IRF-certified guide, BBQ lunch, and transfer from Konjic to the start point at Glavatičevo. Some operators offer package prices if you book multiple activities — for example 45 EUR for a combination with a half-day activity, or 35 EUR for shorter experiences that do not cover the full river section.

Compared with a day of skiing in the Alps or a guided tour in Norway, this is excellent value — especially considering that lunch and all gear are already included.

## Practical tips for international travellers

Pack swimwear to wear under the wetsuit, sunscreen, and a waterproof bag for your phone. The water in the Neretva is cold, especially early in the season, but the wetsuit keeps you warm. If you travel in May or June, bring an extra layer for lunch by the river.

Book ahead, especially in July and August when demand is highest among international visitors. Most operators have English-speaking guides, and many also speak basic German.

End your Balkan trip with a day on the Neretva — it is the kind of experience you will be talking about long after you land back home.
